MARIE CURIE

The woman who unlocked the secrets of radioactivity

Marie Curie (1867–1934) was a pioneering physicist and chemist. She discovered polonium and radium, won Nobel Prizes in both Physics and Chemistry, and revolutionized science and medicine. During World War I, she developed mobile X-ray units for battlefield medical use and inspired generations of scientists, especially women in science.

Here's a time line of Marie Curie's life:

  • 1867 – Born in Warsaw, Poland, as Maria Skłodowska.

  • 1891 – Moves to Paris to attend the Sorbonne University, studying physics and mathematics.

  • 1894 – Meets Pierre Curie, her future husband and research partner.

  • 1895 – Marries Pierre Curie. Together, they begin experiments on uranium rays.

  • 1898 – Discovers the elements polonium (named after her homeland Poland) and radium.

  • 1903 – Receives the Nobel Prize in Physics, shared with Pierre Curie and Henri Becquerel, for work on radioactivity. She becomes the first woman to win a Nobel Prize.

  • 1906 – Pierre Curie dies in a street accident. Marie continues their work alone, taking his place as a professor at the University of Paris, becoming the first female professor there.

  • 1911 – Wins a second Nobel Prize, this time in Chemistry, for her work in isolating pure radium. She is the only person to win Nobel Prizes in two different sciences.

  • 1914-1918 – During World War I, develops mobile X-ray units, called "Little Curies," to assist battlefield doctors.

  • 1920s – Advocates for scientific research, teaches and inspires the next generation of scientists, especially women in science.

  • 1934 – Dies at age 66 from aplastic anemia, caused by prolonged exposure to radioactive materials.

"Nothing in life is to be feared, it is only to be understood. Now is the time to understand more, so that we may fear less."
